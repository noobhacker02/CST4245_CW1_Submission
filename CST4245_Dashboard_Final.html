Hypertension — Going Down

The one good news story: global mean BP fell 5.65pp from 1980–2014, driven by antihypertensive medication access and salt-reduction campaigns in high-income countries.

⚠️
Obesity — Doubled in 35 Years

Global obesity more than doubled. Women consistently higher than men. Pacific Island nations at 59–65% — the majority of their adult populations are clinically obese.

🇦🇪
UAE in the High-Risk Cluster

Female obesity at 41.4%, diabetes at 15.2%. Both place the UAE firmly in the Eastern Mediterranean cluster — higher diabetes per unit of obesity than the global trend predicts.

Key Takeaway: The three conditions are moving in opposite directions — hypertension falling, obesity and diabetes rising. This is not a uniformly worsening picture; it reflects different mechanisms requiring different policy responses.
Chart 1 — Global Prevalence Trends: Hypertension, Obesity & Diabetes (1980–2014)
Mean prevalence across 200 countries per year, split by sex. Each panel has an independent y-axis. Blue solid = Men, Red dashed = Women. Source: NCD-RisC (2016a; 2016b; 2017).
Hypertension ↓ Men 31.9%→26.5%, Women 28.2%→22.3% from 1980 to 2014. Men consistently higher throughout — the opposite of the obesity pattern.
Obesity ↑↑ Women 11.2%→24.0%, Men 5.5%→16.7%. The sex gap widened over time. Female excess concentrated in Pacific Island and Gulf populations.
Diabetes ↑ Near-equal for both sexes. Women marginally higher 1980–2005, then men slightly ahead. Both roughly doubled from ~5% to ~10%.
Chart 2 — Hypertension Gender Gap by Region (Men − Women, %)
Regional mean of Male BP minus Female BP prevalence per year. Below zero = women higher. Dashed line at zero = no gender difference. Source: NCD-RisC (2017); author's own calculation.
Europe — most negative: Women have 7–9pp higher hypertension than men throughout. Iceland, Estonia, Latvia and Czech Republic each exceed −13pp by 2014. Related to dietary salt patterns and detection asymmetry in post-Soviet health systems.
Africa — only region that reversed: Started at −2pp (women higher) in 1980, crossed to +0.24pp (men slightly higher) by 2014. Rising male cardiovascular risk and improving female treatment access both contributed.
Eastern Mediterranean — crossed the line: Started at +0.37pp (men higher) in 1980, then crossed to women-higher around 1997. Sharp rise in female obesity in Gulf countries from the 1990s onward pushed women's cardiovascular risk upward.
Chart 3 — Obesity by Country 2014: Top & Bottom 15
Stacked bars — blue base = Men's rate, red stack = Women's rate added on top. X-axis shows combined male+female total. Countries sorted by average prevalence. Source: NCD-RisC (2016a).
Top 15 — Pacific dominance: Nauru (59% M, 64% F), American Samoa, Cook Islands, Palau. Kuwait and Qatar represent the Gulf cluster. Nauru's rates mean a majority of adults are clinically obese.
Bottom 15 — South/SE Asia + Africa: Vietnam (1.4% M, 2.4% F) sits at the very bottom. Japan is the only high-income country here — its food culture maintains low obesity despite high GDP.
Design note: The x-axis scale difference between the two panels is jarring — the bottom 15 bars barely reach 10% while top 15 combined totals exceed 120%. This is a known limitation of the stacked design chosen to emphasise country-level burden over the sex comparison.
Chart 4 — Obesity vs Diabetes by Country (2014)
Each point = one country. X = mean obesity (both sexes avg), Y = mean diabetes. Coloured by WHO region. Dashed line = linear regression fit. UAE labelled in purple. Sources: NCD-RisC (2016a; 2016b).
Eastern Mediterranean above the line: Higher diabetes per unit of obesity than the regression predicts. Afghanistan: 5% obesity, 12% diabetes. Pakistan: 8% obesity, 12% diabetes. South Asian and Arab populations develop diabetes at lower BMI thresholds (Misra & Khurana, 2011).
East Asian outliers below-left: Japan, South Korea, Vietnam — low obesity AND lower diabetes than comparable income-level countries. China is notably further right than Japan/Korea, reflecting rapid urbanisation-driven diabetes growth since the 1990s.
UAE at (34%, 15.2%): Sits above the regression line, consistent with the Eastern Mediterranean cluster. What the dataset can't separate is how much of this is the large South Asian expat population — national averages mask that demographic split entirely.
Chart 5 — Mean Hypertension Prevalence by Region & Decade (%)
Mean BP averaged across both sexes and all countries per region per decade. Darker red = higher prevalence. Numbers inside cells. Source: NCD-RisC (2017); author's own calculation.
Africa — flat line: 28.6%–29.6% across all four decades. The only region that failed to improve. Low awareness, patchy screening, expensive medication, weak referral pathways (Mills et al., 2016).
Europe & Americas — biggest improvers: Europe 34.3%→25.1%, Americas 28.0%→21.7%. South-East Asia the only region to worsen: 22.8%→24.7%, driven by urbanisation-related cardiovascular risk.
Chart 6 — Global Diabetes Prevalence by Country (2014)
Both sexes mean diabetes prevalence. Orange-red scale — darker = higher. Grey = no boundary match in topojson. Hover for country values. Source: NCD-RisC (2016b).
Middle East — darkest cluster: Egypt, Iraq, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Libya, UAE all above 15%. A geographically contiguous high-risk zone — visible in a way no bar chart could show.
Sub-Saharan Africa — lightest: Mostly below 8%. Reflects lower obesity and younger population age structure. NCD-RisC (2016b) warns this will change substantially as African populations age.
East Asia gradient: China occupies a noticeably different colour band from Japan and South Korea despite being the same region — the diabetes gradient across East Asia is steep enough to be visible at the map level.

Dataset

NCD Risk Factor Collaboration (NCD-RisC). Three sheets: raised blood pressure, BMI/obesity, and age-standardised diabetes prevalence. 200 countries, 1975–2016. Merged on 1980–2014 overlap: 14,000 rows, zero nulls.

Technical Stack

Python 3Pandas Altair / Vega-Lite 5pycountry openpyxlVercel

Data Transformations

Proportions × 100 → percentages. WHO region classification (manual dict). Year restriction to 1980–2014. Gender gap feature: Men − Women per country/year. All documented in cw1_cleaned_dataset.xlsx.
